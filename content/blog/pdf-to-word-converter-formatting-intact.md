You need to edit a PDF — update a number, fix a typo, rework an entire section. But PDFs aren't editable by design. The fastest workaround: convert the PDF to a Word document, make your changes, and export back to PDF if needed.

The catch? Most converters wreck the formatting. Tables break, fonts change, spacing shifts, and you spend more time fixing the Word file than editing it. This guide shows how to convert properly and keep everything intact.

## Why PDF-to-Word Formatting Breaks (And How to Avoid It)

PDFs and Word documents store content in fundamentally different ways:

- **PDF** locks every character at an exact position on the page — it's a layout format, not a document format
- **Word** flows text in paragraphs, wraps columns, and positions elements relative to margins

A converter has to reverse-engineer the Word structure from pixel-perfect PDF positioning. That's why simple tools often produce messy output — they're guessing at the document structure.

**Better converters** analyze the PDF's internal structure: paragraph boundaries, table grids, font metadata, and image layers. The result is a Word file that looks and behaves like the original.

## Converting Scanned PDFs

A scanned PDF is just a stack of images — there's no text to extract. You'll get a Word file with pictures instead of editable text.

**The fix:** Run the PDF through an [OCR tool](/ocr-pdf) first. OCR (Optical Character Recognition) reads the text from the image and creates a real text layer. Then convert the OCR'd PDF to Word — now you get editable text.

Best results come from scans that are:
- At least 200 DPI (300 DPI is ideal)
- Straight, not skewed or rotated
- High contrast (dark text on white background)

## Tips for Best Results

**Use the original PDF, not a printed-and-rescanned version.** A "digital native" PDF (exported from Word, PowerPoint, or a design tool) converts almost perfectly. A PDF that was printed and scanned back in loses structure.

**Check the output before sharing.** Open the Word file, scroll through every page, and verify that tables, images, and formatting look correct. Small fixes take seconds; sending a broken file costs credibility.

**Don't convert and reconvert repeatedly.** Each round trip (PDF → Word → PDF → Word) can introduce small formatting shifts. Make all your edits in one Word session, then do a final PDF export.

**For large files, be patient.** A 100-page PDF with complex tables takes longer than a 5-page letter. The conversion quality is worth the extra seconds.

## FAQ

**Can I convert a password-protected PDF?**

**Does the converter handle non-English text?**
Yes. The tool preserves text in any language — Hindi, Arabic, Chinese, Spanish, and more. Font rendering depends on whether the font is available on your system.

**Can I convert multiple PDFs to Word at once?**
Currently, convert one file at a time. For bulk conversion, upload each file separately — each conversion takes only a few seconds.

**Will the Word file be the same size as the PDF?**
Usually similar, sometimes slightly larger due to Word's file structure. If the resulting `.docx` is too large, you can compress images within Word (File → Compress Pictures).

**Can I convert a PDF form with fillable fields?**
The text content converts, but interactive form fields (checkboxes, dropdowns) become static text. You'll need to recreate the form fields in Word.
